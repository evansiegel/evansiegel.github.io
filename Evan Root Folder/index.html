What is a Collective Bargaining Agreement?

A CBA is a document signed between two parties by collective bargaining. Both the NFL and NBA use the CBA to negotiate terms play, contracts, basic basketball and football operations, and the full context of a league year.

The NBA uses what is called a "soft cap,'' which allows teams to exceed the limit the league allows teams to spend on its players. This is done to both ensure parity, and allow teams in smaller markets like Memphis and Milwaukee to compete with teams in Los Angeles and New York.

What is a salary cap?

The NBA salary cap is a limit on which teams can spend money to acquire players.

Why have a salary cap?

The NBA wants to ensure that all 30 teams have what amounts to the same budget under the NBA rules, giving lifelines to small market teams and make sure that parity exists league-wide.

Has there always been a salary cap?

The NBA used a salary cap during its inaugural 1946--1947 season, but abolished it after just one year. Bob Cousy originally organized the NBA Player's Union in 1954, which eventually led to the first Collective Bargaining Agreement in 1970. The first modern salary cap was used in the 1984-1985 season, set at $3.6 million.

How is the salary cap calculated?

The salary cap is annually calculated as a rough projection of that season's Basketball Related Income. The NBA takes 44.74% of projected Basketball Related Income, subtracts benefits, and then divides the result by the number of teams in the league. Several other adjustments are later made to the total.

What is Basketball Related Income?

There are over two dozen sources of income that contribute to BRI, including:

  • Broadcasting rights
  • Game receipts(including exhibitions)
  • Arena signage proceeds
  • Arena tour proceeds
  • Team promotion proceeds
  • Beverage sale proceeds
  • Team sponsorship proceeds
  • Gambling proceeds
  • Mascot proceeds
  • Parking
